Unlocking Inclusivity: School Messenger App Redesign

Imagine a school communication app designed to simplify life for parents,
teachers, and schools. Sounds like a dream, right? But what if this app left some
parents feeling like they were locked out of the conversation, even when they
wanted to be part of it? That's where my School Messenger App redesign journey
began. Fueled by personal frustration and a determination to improve, I set out to
make his app into a more inclusive and user-friendly tool for all types of families
that is truly accessible and engaging for everyone!

How might we revamp the School Messenger
App to be the ultimate inclusivity champ?

We're talking about making it a communication powerhouse for all parents and
guardians, no matter how their family is shaped.

The Problem

School Messenger App is the bridge between schools and parents, a digital hotline for information exchange, updates, and notifications. Its mission? To help parents stay engaged with their kids and schools like a pro.

But wait, there’s a plot twist! It’s not as inclusive as it should be.

It currently lacks inclusivity for non-traditional families, limiting access to parents whose email addresses are not registered within the school system, even if they are listed as secondary or emergency contacts for a child. This limitation is based on the primary contacts listed during school registration.

In a world where families come in all shapes and sizes, the current app's design and functionality doesn’t quite get the memo. This oversight creates a barrier for many parents and guardians, hindering the app’s goal of fostering parental engagement.

Why do I care?

Picture this: I'm juggling the everyday chaos of parenting in a modern and blended family setup. Now, let's throw in an app that sometimes acts like the gatekeeper of an exclusive club. It’s frustrating being locked out of vital school communication just because my email didn't match some list for some of my children.

And I’m not alone. Many families, like mine, don't fit the cookie-cutter mold. We deserve a communication tool that embraces our diversity and makes life easier, not more complicated - because every parent deserves a stress-free school experience, whether you're in a traditional family, a blended one, or somewhere in between.

Research: Unlocking Insights

To tackle the challenges ahead, I embarked on a journey of exploration using the KWHL table.
It's a bit like putting on my detective hat to uncover the mysteries of the School Messenger
App's user experience.

I scoured Google Play Store reviews, paying attentions to common themes and user concerns.
I also engaged in a semi-structured email interview with a participant who had previously
expressed dissatisfaction with the School Messenger APP. While the conversation wasn't
recorded, we set up a digital Q&A session. I also delved into the academic world with some
good old literature reviews.

What have I LEARNED?

Student success is correlated with the level of parental engagement
to school. However, there is an imbalance with sending and
receiving school communications from both teachers and parents.

Parents are feeling overwhelmed with all the communications and
messages and teachers are finding that often parents are not aware of
them.

There are limitations and restrictions placed on parents and guardians of
certain types of family structures that prevents them from engaging with
the school.

    Reflections: Lessons & Challenges

    Expanding the Definition of Oppression

    In the past, I defined oppression as something obvious and intentional. I never imagined that
    inaccessibility or exclusion in smartphone apps could be a form of oppression. It was a wake-up
    call to realize that something as seemingly mundane as a mobile app could perpetuate bias and
    discrimination. My background in Social Work, with a focus on Anti-Oppressive Study, gave me
    confidence in recognizing privilege and discrimination, but this project challenged my assumptions.
    I discovered that the battle against oppression extends far beyond what's tangible, reaching
    into the digital realm.

    The Impact of Inclusive Design

    My concept of inclusivity was previously limited to tangible aspects like public spaces, employment,
    and housing. Little did I know that our everyday tools, like
    smartphone apps, could also play a role in perpetuating bias and oppression.
    This revelation highlighted the importance of designing with inclusivity in mind. It's not just about addressing individual needs; it's about fostering social inclusivity on a macro
    level. This project opened my eyes to the power of design in creating a fairer, more inclusive digital landscape.
    In fact, inclusivity shouldn't be an afterthought; it should be at the forefront of every design project, promoting equal opportunity and addressing systemic discrimination.

    Designing with User Perspectives

    Designing from a user's perspective is a thought-provoking endeavor. It forces us to step into their shoes
    and consider their needs and wants. But this project took it a step further, urging me to consider not only
    individual goals but also societal inclusivity. It made me realize that as designers, we have the ability to
    shape not just individual experiences but also contribute to a more inclusive society as a whole.

    Challenges of Personal Bias and Emotional Involvement

    One of my biggest struggles in this project was keeping my personal bias in check.
    I chose to redesign an app that I personally use for my children, and my own frustrations
    and emotionscolored my approach. It was a valuable lesson in acknowledging and
    separating personal experiences from the broader societal context. This project
    challenged me to confront my own privilege and reminded me that my narrative is just one of many.

    Injustices in Mainstream Culture

    This project left me with a lasting impression of the importance of awareness. As interaction designers,
    we must be cognizant of discourses surrounding what's perceived as "abnormal" or "unnatural."
    We have a responsibility to design with inclusivity as our guiding framework, not just for
    accommodating different abilities but also for understanding diverse living perspectives. It's
    a small step towards promoting equal opportunity and tackling broader systemic discrimination
    through design.

    Role of Research in Design

    In the process of redesigning the SchoolMessenger app, I've come to appreciate the pivotal role of research.
    It served as our guiding light, revealing user problems, informing design decisions, and uncovering valuable
    insights. This experience underscores the need to dedicate time to understand user needs thoroughly,
    ensuring the success of any redesign project.
